2015 vs 2022: How a Pandemic Reshaped Urban America

Seven years of data reveal the permanent transformation of American cities—from crime patterns to movement behaviors to public health responses

By Jahnavi Mishra
November 1, 2025
15 min read

The Seven-Year Transformation

In 2015, American cities operated under predictable rhythms. Crime followed seasonal patterns. People moved freely between work, home, and recreation. Public health crises were theoretical concerns studied in textbooks, not lived experiences that reshaped daily life.

By 2022, everything had changed. A global pandemic didn't just pause urban life—it permanently rewired it. This investigation analyzes seven years of data across four dimensions: crime incidents in Boston, mobility patterns nationwide, vaccination distribution, and the global spread of COVID-19 cases.

2015: The Before Times

  • Crime patterns followed decades-old seasonal trends
  • Urban mobility remained constant and predictable
  • Public health infrastructure operated in routine mode
  • Global connectivity faced no major disruptions

2022: The New Normal

  • Crime rates showed unprecedented pandemic disruptions
  • Mobility behaviors permanently shifted toward remote work
  • Vaccination became the defining public health challenge
  • Global pandemic reshaped international movement

The data tells a story of adaptation, disruption, and transformation. This is not just about numbers—it's about understanding how a global crisis permanently altered the fabric of urban American life.

Chapter One

The Crime Paradox: When Lockdowns Changed Everything

Boston's crime data from 2015 to 2022 reveals something unexpected: the pandemic didn't just pause criminal activity—it fundamentally disrupted patterns that had held steady for decades.

View Interactive Download Data

Monthly crime distribution across Boston police districts, 2015-2022. Source: Boston Police Department Crime Incident Reports

Key Insights

Seasonal Pattern: Crime incidents increase during summer months (May–August) and drop in winter (November–February), suggesting seasonal influence on criminal activity.

District Variation: Districts such as B2, C11, D4, and A1 consistently show higher incident volumes, while A15, E5, and E18 report fewer crimes, reflecting variation in population density and urban activity.

COVID-19 Impact: A notable dip in 2020 aligns with COVID-19 lockdowns, when reduced movement led to fewer recorded crimes.

Stable Trends: Trends remain stable across years, indicating persistent district-level patterns rather than random fluctuations.

Alternative Visualizations

Heatmap: Plot months on X-axis and districts on Y-axis with color intensity showing crime counts for quick pattern recognition.

Stacked Area Chart: Display total crimes across all districts, highlighting each district's share in the overall volume.

Interactive Dashboard: Allow users to select specific districts or years to compare detailed monthly trends.

Geospatial Map: Use a Boston district map shaded by total crime counts to visualize spatial concentration alongside temporal trends.

Drawbacks & Limitations

Independent Y-axes make it difficult to compare absolute crime levels across districts directly.

Small multiple layout can appear crowded, especially with many districts, reducing readability.

Monthly aggregation hides day-to-day fluctuations or yearly progressions in total crime levels.

Visualization does not account for district population or area size, which can bias interpretations based on raw counts.

"Crime didn't disappear in 2020—it transformed. The question is whether those changes are permanent."

Chapter Two

Movement Transformed: The Mobility Revolution of 2022

By 2022, Americans were moving again—but not the way they used to. Google's mobility data reveals a nation that learned to live differently.

View Interactive Download Data

Average change in mobility across the United States, 2022. Source: Google COVID-19 Community Mobility Reports

Key Insights

Overall Recovery: Mobility trends across the U.S. in 2022 indicate a return toward pre-pandemic norms, with most categories stabilizing around baseline.

Omicron Dip (Feb): A sharp fall across transit, workplaces, and retail marks the Omicron wave's short-term impact.

Spring Rise (Apr–May): Clear rebounds in parks, grocery, and recreation activities reflect renewed outdoor and economic movement.

Summer & Fall Variation: Parks show seasonal peaks, while residential mobility rises again during the September holiday period.

Stabilization by Q4: By October, all mobility categories flatten, showing the U.S. transition to a post-pandemic steady state.

Alternative Visualizations

Stacked Area Chart: To display how each mobility type contributes to overall movement change through time.

Heatmap Calendar: Shows daily intensity of change per variable, highlighting peaks and dips visually.

Small Multiples Grid: Individual mini-charts per category (similar to Boston crime grid) for cleaner comparison.

Interactive Dashboard: Combine sliders and filters to compare multiple countries or time ranges side by side.

Drawbacks & Limitations

Overlapping Lines: With six series on one chart, smaller variations can be hard to isolate visually.

Daily Volatility: Day-to-day noise obscures long-term patterns; weekly averages could make trends smoother.

Context Gaps: The chart doesn't show external drivers (e.g., fuel prices, weather, policy timelines) that affect mobility.

Annotation Density: Too many event markers may clutter the plot on smaller screens; limiting to 3–4 key events would improve clarity.

"We didn't return to 2019. We created something new—a hybrid existence that blends physical and digital life in ways we're still learning to navigate."

Chapter Three

The Vaccine Divide: Geographic Patterns in Public Health

The vaccination rollout revealed something uncomfortable: America's public health infrastructure follows population density, not per-capita need.

View Interactive Download Data

Total COVID-19 vaccinations by state. Source: U.S. Census Bureau & summitpost.org

Key Insights

Population Correlation: The visualization shows vaccination totals across the U.S., with darker shades representing states that have administered more vaccinations.

High-Volume States: States like California, Texas, Florida, and New York are the darkest, reflecting their large populations and high vaccination counts.

Low-Volume States: Lighter shades represent smaller population states such as Wyoming, Vermont, and Montana, which naturally have fewer total vaccinations.

Geographic Pattern: The map highlights how vaccination distribution largely follows population density patterns.

Quick Overview: This visualization effectively provides a quick geographic overview of vaccination totals across the country.

Is binned color scheme appropriate?

Yes, the binned color scheme is appropriate for this visualization because it allows clear comparison between states by grouping them into distinct value ranges. It simplifies interpretation — users can easily distinguish which states fall into low, medium, or high vaccination categories.

However, a continuous color scheme could be used if the goal were to show subtle differences in vaccination numbers across states. Continuous gradients are useful when changes are gradual and not categorical.

Alternative Visualizations

Normalized Choropleth Map: Use vaccination rate (% of population) instead of total counts — gives a more accurate representation of vaccine coverage.

Bar Chart (Horizontal or Sorted): Compare states ranked by vaccination totals or rates for clearer quantitative differences.

Bubble Map Overlay: Combine color (rate) and bubble size (total count) for dual insight — both scale and proportion.

Heatmap by Region and Time: Show how vaccination coverage evolved weekly or monthly for each region.

Interactive Dashboard: Include a map and a linked bar chart — selecting a state highlights its trend or rank.

Drawbacks & Limitations

The visualization shows total vaccination counts, which correlate strongly with population size — larger states appear more vaccinated even if their rate is average.

The binned color scheme, while visually clear, may oversimplify differences between states that fall within the same bin.

The map does not reflect vaccination coverage (percentage), which would provide a fairer comparison across states.

Missing or unclassified areas (like U.S. territories or federal entities) may appear blank or misleading.

Spatial maps are visually appealing but less precise for numeric comparison than bar or line charts.

Chapter Four

The Global Cascade: How COVID Spread Across Borders

The virus didn't respect borders, timezones, or political systems. This animated timeline shows how confirmed cases exploded across continents, revealing which nations were hit first—and which paid the highest price.

View Interactive Download Data

Top 10 countries by cumulative COVID-19 confirmed cases, 2020-present. Source: Johns Hopkins University CSSE COVID-19 Data Repository

Key Insights

Dominance of the U.S.: The United States consistently leads in cumulative confirmed cases throughout the timeline, surpassing 80 million cases by 2022.

India's Second-Wave Surge: A sharp rise in India's numbers during mid-2021 reflects the Delta variant outbreak, pushing it past 40 million cases.

Emerging European Peaks: France, the U.K., and Germany exhibit repeated surges aligning with Omicron and earlier winter waves.

Latin America Early Impact: Brazil shows strong early growth in 2020–2021, becoming one of the top three due to prolonged high transmission rates.

Shifts Over Time: Rankings change as later-wave countries (e.g., South Korea and Turkey) overtake early hotspots, illustrating the evolving geography of infection.

Alternative Visualizations

Stacked Area Chart: To show cumulative global totals and regional contributions simultaneously.

Interactive World Map (Choropleth): To emphasize geographic spread and shifting intensity by month.

Small-Multiple Line Charts: One per continent or top-N country, making wave timing clearer.

Animated Radial Timeline: Circular growth showing global progression month-by-month.

Drawbacks & Limitations

Cumulative view hides wave patterns — month-to-month acceleration or decline is not easily visible.

Ranking compression — smaller countries' differences vanish as top nations dominate the scale.

No per-capita normalization — populous countries naturally appear worse, regardless of infection rate.

Update sensitivity — cumulative data lags real-time trends, so short-term policy effects aren't clear.

Flag visuals — appealing but can distract from quantitative comparison if over-used or inconsistent in size.

"Pandemics don't end. They transform into endemic patterns we learn to live with—or fail to."

Methodology & Data Sources

Data Collection

This investigation synthesizes four distinct datasets spanning 2015-2022: Boston crime incident reports from the Boston Police Department, Google COVID-19 Community Mobility Reports averaged across U.S. states, vaccination data from the U.S. Census Bureau and summitpost.org, and global confirmed case counts from Johns Hopkins University CSSE COVID-19 Data Repository.

Analytical Approach

Crime data was analyzed using monthly aggregations across police districts to identify seasonal patterns and pandemic-related disruptions. Mobility trends employed percentage change from baseline (pre-pandemic levels) to normalize for state population differences. Vaccination data was mapped using total counts, though analysis notes the limitations of absolute numbers versus per-capita rates. Global case data was tracked daily from 2020 onwards, capturing real-time pandemic progression and variant-driven waves across countries.

Visualization Methods

All interactive visualizations were created using Flourish Studio, enabling dynamic exploration of temporal patterns. Small multiples facilitated district-level crime comparisons. Multi-line charts showed overlapping mobility categories to reveal correlation patterns. Choropleth maps provided geographic context for vaccination distribution. Bar chart races animated the global progression of confirmed cases, making temporal shifts immediately apparent.

Limitations

Crime data reflects only reported incidents and may undercount during periods of reduced police-civilian interaction. Mobility data represents opt-in Google users, potentially skewing toward certain demographics. Vaccination totals correlate strongly with state population, obscuring per-capita coverage rates. Global case counts depend on testing capacity and reporting transparency, which varied dramatically by country and time period.

Data Processing

Federal entities, territories, and unclassified geographic categories were excluded from state-level vaccination mapping to maintain consistency with Flourish's geographic boundaries. All data underwent cleaning to remove duplicates, correct formatting errors, and standardize temporal granularity to monthly intervals for cross-dataset comparison.
